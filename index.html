01 - Abstract

Abstract

Humans don't reread the same textbook ten times. Why should language models?

Training modern language models is expensive and repetitive. Models read the same data over and over, sometimes dozens of times, to learn effectively. This is nothing like how humans learn.

We take inspiration from sleep. When humans sleep, the brain revisits difficult experiences, not only storing them but processing them more thoroughly until they stick. We designed a training pipeline that works the same way: rather than repeatedly looping over all the data, the model works through it in chunks, and between each chunk, it "sleeps" by replaying and reprocessing the samples it struggled with most, giving difficult material extra attention before moving on.

We tested this on the RoBERTa model using the 100M-token BabyLM corpus, a dataset designed to mimic the amount of language a child receives by age 13.

02 - Introduction

Introduction

Modern language models are powerful, but training them from scratch is enormously expensive. Building an effective model requires exposing it to far more text than any human being will encounter in an entire lifetime. BERT and ELMo were each trained on billions of words, RoBERTa on 30 billion, and the Llama family on tens of trillions. By comparison, developmental psychologists estimate that a child hears roughly 3 to 11 million words per year. Language models, in other words, consume the linguistic equivalent of hundreds of thousands of years of human experience before they can reliably comprehend a sentence.

Part of this gap is structural. Standard training exposes the model to the same dataset tens to thousands of times. Every epoch, every sentence is revisited at equal attention, regardless of whether the model has already learned from it. This is computationally wasteful: once a model has mastered easy examples, re-training on them adds negligible signal while consuming real compute. And it is cognitively implausible. Humans do not relive every experience from childhood. We move through time once, and we remember.

What we do have is sleep. Neuroscientists have established that sleep is an active process of memory consolidation, not mere rest. While the waking brain is optimized for encoding new experiences, sleep provides an off-line environment in which the brain selectively replays recent, difficult, or surprising experiences, gradually stabilizing them into long-term synaptic networks. This two-stage model, in which rapid encoding during waking is followed by consolidation during sleep, is thought to be central to how humans acquire complex cognitive skills, including language, from comparatively limited input.

Sleep also performs a kind of neural housekeeping. During the day, synapses broadly strengthen as information is encoded, a process that is metabolically costly and accumulates noise. During sleep, the brain downscales weaker connections while preserving and reinforcing the strongest, through a process called synaptic homeostasis. The effect is a leaner, more generalized memory system, cleared of spurious associations and ready for the next day's learning.

We believe this is a principle worth importing into machine learning. Prior sleep-inspired work has focused mainly on catastrophic forgetting, using replay to preserve old knowledge as models learn new tasks. We take a different angle. Rather than fighting forgetting, we ask whether sleep-like consolidation can improve sample efficiency during pretraining itself. What happens if a language model is trained the way humans learn, processing each sample only once but pausing between data blocks to consolidate what was hardest?

04 - Methods

Methods

Dataset. We train on the BabyLM 100M word corpus, a benchmark designed to approximate the linguistic input a child receives by age 13. The corpus blends two domains: transcribed speech (roughly 56%), drawn from movie and TV subtitles, educational video transcripts, adult-child conversation recordings, and telephone dialogues; and child-directed written language (44%), including simplified encyclopedias, children's books, and literary texts. This composition reflects how hearing children actually encounter language: predominantly through spoken interaction, supplemented by reading.

Model. We use a small RoBERTa-PreLayerNorm model with 8 hidden layers, 8 attention heads, a hidden size of 256, and an intermediate size of 2048. The compact scale allows us to run thorough hyperparameter sweeps under a realistic compute budget while still capturing meaningful training dynamics.

Sleep-Consolidated Training. The core of our approach replaces the standard multi-epoch training loop with a sequence of alternating wake and sleep phases, repeated for a fixed number of cycles. The full dataset is partitioned into equally-sized folds, one per cycle.

During a wake phase, the model processes a single fold exactly once, training on the masked language modeling objective. As it trains, we record the cross-entropy loss for every sample. No sample is revisited within the wake phase.

At the end of each wake phase, we populate a replay buffer by sampling from the processed data using loss-weighted random selection: samples that the model struggled with more are more likely to be retained. The proportion of data retained is controlled by the replay ratio.

During the sleep phase, the model stops receiving new data and trains exclusively on the replay buffer. Before training begins, the buffered samples are shuffled and recombined into contiguous token blocks, then re-split at artificial boundaries rather than original sample boundaries. This context-augmented padding, adapted from prior work on contextualizer pretraining, prevents the model from memorizing surface patterns and encourages more abstract generalization. The sleep phase runs until a target loss threshold is reached or a maximum step count is exceeded, at which point the replay buffer is emptied.

After each sleep phase, the data from that wake cycle is discarded. Each sample in the full dataset is seen at most once during training.

Ablation Studies. To isolate the contribution of each component, we test three ablation conditions: replacing difficulty-based replay with random sample selection; setting the replay ratio to 1.0, so the sleep phase trains on all wake data rather than a difficult subset; and disabling context-augmented padding entirely, training the sleep phase on the original sample order.

Evaluation. We compare against a multi-epoch baseline matched by total training steps rather than epochs, ensuring a fair, compute-controlled comparison. Beyond training perplexity, we evaluate on the Benchmark of Linguistic Minimal Pairs (BLiMP) to probe grammatical acceptability and syntactic knowledge, and on selected GLUE tasks for semantic generalization. We also track sleep-specific metrics: the standard deviation and maximum of per-sample perplexity within batches, to examine whether sleep phases are successfully narrowing the gap between easy and hard data points over time.
